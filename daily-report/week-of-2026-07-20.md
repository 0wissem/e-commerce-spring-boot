## Monday 2026-07-20 — System Design fundamentals: metrics, trade-offs, CAP theorem

### What I did

- **Shifted from interview-drill mode to System Design / Architecture.** The goal: learn to think like a software architect, not just pass an interview round. Started with foundational concepts that apply to every system.

- **Mastered the four core metrics and their trade-offs.** Every system optimizes for some combination of: (1) **latency** (how fast), (2) **throughput** (how many concurrent), (3) **availability** (always up), (4) **consistency** (correct/fresh data). The constraint: you pick two, sacrifice one. Worked through four real scenarios to internalize this:
  - **Product search** (millions/day, prices change hourly) → prioritize throughput, sacrifice consistency (10s stale is OK)
  - **Payment system** (must be accurate) → prioritize consistency, sacrifice throughput (fewer concurrent payments, but each is correct)
  - **Notifications** (millions of users, must be always up) → prioritize availability, sacrifice consistency (notifications might dupe/reorder)
  - **Analytics dashboard** (scan millions, updates daily) → prioritize throughput, sacrifice both latency (30s queries acceptable) and consistency (1-day stale expected)

- **Internalized the pattern:** constraints are natural (what your system needs to handle). Name the constraint → identify which metric matters → sacrifice the least-important one. This is the architect's core skill — not how to implement, but what to trade off.

- **Introduced CAP theorem:** in a distributed system (multiple servers), you have at most 2 of 3 — **Consistency, Availability, Partition tolerance**. Since network failures always happen (partition is inevitable), you really choose between consistency and availability. During a network split: stop accepting writes (consistency) or keep running with diverging data (availability). You can't have both.

### Problems and how I resolved them

- **Confused by "pick two, sacrifice one"** initially — treated it as abstract rules rather than concrete trade-offs tied to real constraints. Resolved by working through scenarios and asking "what does this system actually need?" — the constraint reveals what to sacrifice.

### Key takeaways

- **Every system has constraints; constraints dictate trade-offs.** Don't optimize for all four metrics — identify what the system actually needs and sacrifice wisely.
- **Consistency and availability are opposites under network failures.** You can't have both when servers can't talk to each other. Pick one.
- **The architect's job is deciding what to trade off, not how to implement.** Engineers build; architects decide what to sacrifice.
- **Patterns repeat across systems.** Product search, notifications, analytics, payments — each teaches the same principle in a different context.
